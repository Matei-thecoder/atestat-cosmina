History and Origin

The tulip is native to Central Asia and was first cultivated by the Turks as early as 1000 AD. It was later introduced to Europe through the Ottoman Empire. The tulip became particularly famous in the Netherlands during the 17th century, during a period known as "Tulip Mania," when the flower's popularity caused prices to soar dramatically, becoming one of the first recorded economic bubbles.

Botanical Characteristics

Tulips belong to the Liliaceae family. There are around 75 wild species and over 3,000 varieties that have been cultivated. Their distinctive cup-shaped blooms come in almost every color of the rainbow. Most tulips bloom in the spring, and they thrive in climates with cool winters and dry summers, making them a favorite in gardens worldwide.

Symbolism and Cultural Impact

Throughout history, tulips have symbolized perfect love, rebirth, and charity. Red tulips represent deep love, while yellow tulips once meant hopeless love but now commonly symbolize cheerfulness. In Persian literature, tulips are associated with the beauty of life and martyrdom, appearing frequently in poetry and art.
